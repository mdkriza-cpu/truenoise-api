Methodology

How we measure aircraft noise

TrueNoise measures community aircraft noise impact using a calibrated iPhone-based system. This page documents exactly how the measurements are made, so that the data can be evaluated, replicated, and challenged on its merits.

Bottom line

Our system has been validated against a TA657A Type 2 reference sound level meter during a live Airbus A320 overflight. The two instruments agreed within 1.4 dBA on peak SPL — well inside the ±2 dB unit-to-unit tolerance that IEC 61672 permits between Class 2 meters. This was a real outdoor measurement with wind and ambient noise, not a controlled laboratory tone.

We measure at the receptor — where people actually live and sleep — because that is the measurement that matters for health outcomes. WHO, EPA, and ICAO noise-impact research all use receptor-based exposure, not source-based emission.

Section 1

Hardware

Microphone

  • ModelPoP Voice Professional Lavalier omnidirectional condenser
  • Frequency range20 Hz – 16 kHz
  • Signal-to-noise ratio80 dB
  • ConnectionWired (no Bluetooth codec compression or variable latency)
  • Wind protectionFoam + fur windshield (per ISO 1996 guidance)
  • Polar patternOmnidirectional

The frequency range fully covers the A-weighted measurement band: A-weighting rolls off by ~50 dB below 20 Hz and ~7 dB above 16 kHz, so this microphone captures everything the A-weighting curve cares about. The omnidirectional pattern is deliberate — aircraft can fly in from any bearing, and equal sensitivity in all directions avoids the orientation bias a cardioid pattern would introduce.

This is a consumer-grade microphone, not an IEC 61672-certified measurement microphone. We address that directly in §6 Calibration and §8 Limitations.

Phone

  • DeviceApple iPhone with A-series silicon
  • Audio session mode.measurement (disables all processing — see §2)
  • CalibrationPer-device offset stored locally and applied to every measurement
Section 2

iOS audio capture

We use Apple's AVAudioEngine framework with the audio session category set to .playAndRecord and mode set to .measurement.

This mode is the critical detail. By default, iOS aggressively processes microphone input for voice calls and video — automatic gain control, noise suppression, voice isolation, and echo cancellation are all applied. None of these are acceptable for an instrument. .measurement mode disables all of them, exposing the raw uncompressed PCM signal exactly as the microphone capsule delivers it.

  • Tap buffer size1024 frames (~21 ms at 48 kHz)
  • Sample format32-bit float
  • Sample rateHardware-native (typically 48 kHz)

Critics sometimes assume "phone" implies "consumer audio pipeline." With .measurement mode, it does not.

Section 3

A-weighting filter

We implement A-weighting per IEC 61672, the international standard for sound level meters.

The filter is constructed as three cascaded biquad sections derived via bilinear transform from the analog s-domain transfer function. The four canonical IEC pole frequencies are used directly:

  • f₁20.598997 Hz
  • f₂107.65265 Hz
  • f₃737.86223 Hz
  • f₄12194.217 Hz

Section 1 is a 2nd-order high-pass at f₁. Section 2 is a 2nd-order high-pass combining the f₂ and f₃ poles. Section 3 is a 2nd-order low-pass at f₄. The overall response is normalized so the gain at 1 kHz is exactly 0 dB — the IEC reference point.

These are the same pole frequencies used in every certified sound level meter manufactured. A-weighting compensates for the frequency response of the human ear, which is roughly 30 dB less sensitive at 100 Hz than at 1 kHz. Measuring aircraft noise without A-weighting would dramatically overstate low-frequency content that humans don't perceive as loud.

Section 4

FFT and ⅓-octave band analysis

A-weighted SPL is the headline number, but it collapses the entire audible spectrum into a single decibel value. For finer analysis, we compute a ⅓-octave spectrum on every measurement window.

  • FFT engineApple Accelerate / vDSP — the same SIMD-optimized DSP library Apple uses in Logic Pro and that ships in every professional iOS audio plugin
  • FFT size4096 samples (~85 ms window at 48 kHz)
  • Window functionHann window with energy normalization — band levels are calibrated in absolute SPL, not arbitrary spectral units
  • Frequency resolution~11.7 Hz per bin
  • Bands28 standard ⅓-octave bands, 25 Hz – 12.5 kHz (ANSI S1.11 / IEC 61260 center frequencies)
  • Band powerSummed across all FFT bins within each band's bounds (edge factor 2^(1/6))

The A-series chip's vector and neural processing capabilities exceed what real-time spectral analysis requires by orders of magnitude. A 4096-point FFT executes in well under a millisecond. Doubts about "can a phone do FFT accurately" reflect assumptions about pre-2010 phone hardware.

Section 5

Psychoacoustic analysis

A-weighted SPL is a 1936 model of how humans perceive loudness. It is the regulatory standard worldwide, but it is known to be incomplete — particularly for impulsive sounds, tonal components, and the spectral character that makes some noises feel sharper or more annoying than their dBA value would suggest.

We compute, on top of A-weighting, three psychoacoustic metrics from the acoustics literature:

  • Zwicker loudness (sone)Simplified ISO 532 B. Computes specific loudness per Bark critical band, integrates across the Bark scale. Uses ISO 226 threshold-in-quiet values per band.
  • Loudness level (phon)Derived from total loudness in sones. Numerically equals the dB SPL of a 1 kHz tone perceived as equally loud.
  • Sharpness (acum)Per DIN 45692. Weights high-frequency specific loudness to capture the perceptual difference between a low rumble and a piercing whine at the same dBA.
  • Psychoacoustic annoyanceZwicker's combined model synthesizing loudness and sharpness into a single annoyance index.

These metrics matter because two aircraft producing the same dBA can produce very different real-world annoyance — and annoyance is the dose-response variable that WHO uses for sleep disturbance and cardiovascular health endpoints. We provide psychoacoustic analysis in addition to A-weighted SPL, not instead of it.

Section 6

Calibration

Every iPhone unit, microphone unit, and microphone connection has slightly different acoustic gain. A measurement system that does not address this is not a measurement system.

We calibrate per device. The app stores a calibration offset (in dB) that converts the device's raw signal level to absolute SPL. Two offset profiles are supported:

  • Internal mic offset+108 dB (averaged across multiple calibration sessions on the validation iPhone)
  • External mic offset+99 dB (PoP Voice lavalier, averaged similarly)
  • Session-to-session variability±3 dB observed — offset is averaged across multiple calibration sessions
  • Reference instrumentTA657A Type 2 sound level meter

The offset is determined by simultaneous measurement against a reference instrument and is stored locally in the app. This is the same approach used by any professional sound level meter: every unit is individually calibrated, and calibration is checked periodically against a reference.

Windshield insertion loss characterization

Windshields attenuate real acoustic signal in addition to suppressing wind noise — more material in the signal path means more signal loss, particularly at high frequencies where wavelengths approach the windshield's structural dimensions. This attenuation has been characterized by simultaneous comparison against the TA657A reference meter across multiple noise types (broadband, tonal, impulsive):

Configuration Measured insertion loss Published industry range Notes
Bare mic (no windshield) 0.0 dBA 0 dB reference Matched TA657A across all noise types
Foam windshield 0.6–0.8 dBA 0.5–1.0 dBA Within published band (Rycote, Brüel & Kjær)
Fur windshield (deadcat) 1.8 dBA 1.5–3.0 dBA Used for all outdoor monitoring sessions

The fact that insertion loss is consistent across all noise types confirms that windshield attenuation is broadly frequency-flat across the audible band, as expected from published specifications. Different source spectra all see similar dBA reductions — there are no frequency-dependent surprises in the measurement chain.

All outdoor monitoring sessions are conducted with the fur windshield installed. Published measurements therefore carry a known conservative correction of 1.8 dBA — actual noise levels at the receptor are 1.8 dB higher than reported values. This correction will be applied automatically in future dataset versions once per-session windshield configuration is logged by the app. Researchers requiring the corrected values may apply the 1.8 dBA offset to all current outdoor observations.

The bare mic result — matching the TA657A perfectly across multiple noise types with no offset adjustment — is the cleanest possible confirmation that the +99 dB external mic calibration offset is correct, stable, and free of drift.

Expanded validation dataset

Subsequent calibration sessions have expanded the validation dataset with additional simultaneous comparisons against the TA657A Type 2 reference meter. At a standardized receptor position (1.2 m height, 1.5 m from façade, concrete patio, fur windshield with characterized correction applied), three 737 takeoff overflights produced an average app-vs-meter delta of +1.4 dBA (range 0.6–1.9 dBA) — well within the ±2 dB IEC 61672 Class 2 unit-to-unit tolerance.

Same-session controlled bench tests against three reference noise sources revealed a small spectral sensitivity bias in the microphone:

Reference source App vs TA657A delta Notes
White noise (flat spectrum)−0.6 dBAHigh-frequency content
Pink noise (1/f spectrum)+0.6 dBABalanced spectrum
Brown noise (low-frequency)+1.1 dBALow-frequency emphasis
737 takeoff overflight+1.4 dBA (avg)Jet exhaust mixing noise — heavily low-frequency

The ~1.7 dBA spread from high- to low-frequency reference sources reflects a modest low-frequency sensitivity bias in the external microphone. The 737 takeoff result (+1.4 dBA) is consistent with this bias — jet exhaust mixing noise is heavily low-frequency-dominated, and the microphone reads slightly high for low-frequency content. This is an honest characterization of the system's spectral behavior, not a calibration failure. All comparisons fall within Class 2 tolerance.

Combined with the prior A320 approach result and the windshield insertion-loss characterization, the validation dataset now spans two aircraft types, two flight phases (approach and takeoff), two microphones (internal and external), and three controlled spectral references. All comparisons agree within Class 2 tolerance.

Boundary reinforcement — receptor positioning

A separate set of measurements with the microphone resting on a vehicle door surface produced app-vs-meter deltas of approximately +3.8 dBA. These were identified as boundary-coupling artifacts from microphone placement — the hard surface acts as an acoustic reflector, adding energy to the measurement — and are not treated as calibration data. They instead empirically demonstrate the ~3 dB boundary reinforcement effect that motivates the standardized receptor position used in all TrueNoise field sessions: 1.2 m height, 1.5 m from any reflective surface, held or mounted free of contact with hard structures.

Section 6b

Measurement position classification

Not all measurement positions are acoustically equivalent. A microphone on a tripod in an open field and a microphone clipped to a bedroom window sill produce meaningfully different readings from the same aircraft — and both readings are correct for their respective purposes. TrueNoise records the acoustic geometry of each session so that analysts can apply appropriate interpretation.

Each session is assigned one of five Measurement Type values, which define the acoustic geometry rather than the user's identity or intent:

Measurement Type Geometry Primary use
Standardized Receptor Tripod, ≥1 m from all walls and surfaces, 1.2 m height Regulatory comparison; ISO 1996 compliant; directly comparable to airport noise contour modeling
Community Receptor Where someone actually lives — porch, balcony, back patio, fire escape Lived experience; includes +3 to +6 dB boundary reinforcement that residents actually experience; health impact assessment
Facade-Level 0.5–2 m from a single reflective wall Sleep-disturbance and indoor-exposure prediction; matches WHO L_DEN methodology for façade measurement
Field Characterization Mobile or temporary position at a strategic location Corridor mapping, approach/departure track characterization, comparative site surveys
Hand-held No fixed mount Opportunistic observations; higher positional variability; useful for event detection, not absolute SPL

A free-form Position Description field accompanies each session, capturing the specific setup: for example, "Tripod, 1.5 m, middle of patio" or "Mic clipped to bedroom window sill exterior." This allows future analysts to reconstruct the measurement geometry precisely.

The distinction between Standardized Receptor and Community Receptor is particularly important for advocacy contexts. A Standardized Receptor reading is the appropriate comparison against regulatory thresholds and airport noise contour models — both of which assume free-field conditions. A Community Receptor reading reflects what residents actually hear and what their bodies actually respond to, including the boundary reinforcement from nearby hard surfaces. Both are valid. Neither should be substituted for the other without acknowledgment.

Wind contamination screening — C-A Delta

A-weighting and C-weighting

Sound level meters apply a frequency-weighting filter to raw acoustic measurements to better reflect how the measurement should be interpreted. Two standardized weightings are relevant here, both defined in IEC 61672:

  • A-weighting (dBA)Approximates how the human ear perceives loudness at typical environmental sound levels. Heavily attenuates frequencies below 500 Hz and slightly attenuates very high frequencies. Used in nearly all environmental noise regulation — FAA contours, WHO guidelines, EPA standards.
  • C-weighting (dBC)Much closer to a flat frequency response across the audible band — includes far more low-frequency content than A-weighting. Used in peak sound pressure standards, occupational noise assessment, and as a diagnostic complement to A-weighting.

The TrueNoise app computes both A-weighted and C-weighted levels in parallel from every audio sample, using textbook IEC 61672 biquad filter implementations (3 cascaded sections for A-weighting, 2 for C-weighting, both bilinear-transformed from the analog s-domain specification).

Why the C-A delta detects wind

Wind noise on a microphone is not acoustic — it is turbulent air pressure directly on the diaphragm. This pressure noise is overwhelmingly low-frequency, typically concentrated below 200 Hz. A-weighting heavily suppresses energy below 200 Hz; C-weighting does not. The consequence:

  • Aircraft, traffic, voice, environmental sourcesMost audible energy is in bands where A and C respond similarly. LC − LA typically 5–15 dB.
  • Wind pressure on diaphragmAlmost all energy is in low-frequency bands C-weighting captures but A-weighting suppresses. LC − LA typically 25–50 dB.

A large C-A delta during a meaningful acoustic signal is therefore a strong indicator of wind contamination rather than genuine acoustic content. This technique is used in professional sound level meters and referenced in ISO 1996 measurement procedure.

Interpretation thresholds and the SPL floor

For each 2-second peak measurement, both A-weighted and C-weighted peak levels are recorded and the delta computed. The thresholds below apply only when the A-weighted level is at or above 55 dBA — that is, during a meaningful acoustic event such as an aircraft overflight:

  • C-A Delta < 15 dBClean — measurement is dominated by genuine acoustic content; no wind concern
  • C-A Delta 15–25 dBPossible wind contamination — flag for review; consider excluding from SPL analysis
  • C-A Delta ≥ 25 dBStrong wind contamination — exclude from SPL analysis by default

This SPL floor is important. When the microphone captures only the local ambient environment — no aircraft, no nearby vehicle, no speech — the signal is dominated by low-frequency ambient content: distant traffic, HVAC, building hum, infrastructure rumble. This naturally produces large C-A deltas (often 15–25+ dB) because C-weighting captures low-frequency energy that A-weighting suppresses. A large C-A delta during quiet ambient conditions is not wind contamination — it is information about the location's low-frequency acoustic character.

Field data from a standardized receptor session (18 May 2026) illustrates this directly: aircraft overflight peaks at 70+ dBA showed C-A deltas of 4–6 dB (clean signal, genuine acoustic content), while quiet ambient samples at 45 dBA showed C-A deltas of 18–20 dB — not wind contamination, but the bass-heavy character of the suburban acoustic environment at that location.

All outdoor sessions use a fur windshield (characterized insertion loss: 1.8 dBA), which suppresses wind noise significantly. The C-A Delta provides an objective, per-observation quality flag that complements the windshield's passive protection. Observations flagged by C-A Delta are retained in the dataset with their flag value; analysts may apply their own thresholds as appropriate for their use case.

Glossary

  • A-weighting (dBA)A frequency filter applied to measurements to match human hearing sensitivity; used in nearly all environmental noise regulations.
  • C-weighting (dBC)A nearly-flat frequency filter that includes more low-frequency content; used as a diagnostic complement to A-weighting.
  • C-A Delta (dB)The difference between a C-weighted and A-weighted measurement of the same signal; large values during loud measurements (≥55 dBA) indicate wind contamination.

Background baseline percentiles — forthcoming

The TrueNoise iOS app now computes session-level background acoustic statistics — LAeq, LA10, LA50, LA90, and LA95 — derived from the population of 2-second peak samples captured during a session. These percentiles characterize the ambient acoustic environment at the measurement location independent of aircraft events: LA90 approximates the background acoustic floor, LA50 the median level, and LA10 the level exceeded only during the noisiest 10% of the session.

This baseline data is currently retained locally in the iOS app and is not yet uploaded to the TrueNoise database. It will be incorporated into the public dataset once sufficient sessions have been captured at known locations under controlled conditions to validate the percentile estimates against expected acoustic environments. Methodology for interpreting and presenting these values will be published alongside the data.

Section 7

Validation

On 9 May 2026, the system was compared against a TA657A Type 2 sound level meter during a clearly identified Airbus A320 overflight at a documented residential location in Severn, Maryland:

Instrument Peak SPL Notes
TA657A (Type 2 reference) 72.4 dBA IEC 61672 Class 2
TrueNoise app (external mic, +99 dB offset) 71.0 dBA PoP Voice lavalier

IEC 61672 permits ±2 dB of unit-to-unit variation between two Class 2 instruments measuring the same signal. Our system agreed with the reference within that tolerance, in real outdoor conditions with wind and ambient noise — not a controlled tone in an anechoic chamber.

This is one validated comparison. We are continuing to collect comparison data points across different aircraft types and weather conditions, and we will publish the running table as it grows.

Section 8

Limitations

Honest disclosure is part of credibility. The following are known limitations of the current system:

1
The microphone is not IEC 61672 certified. It is a consumer omnidirectional condenser with a manufacturer-published frequency range but no individual calibration certificate or temperature-stability specification. We rely on empirical validation against the TA657A rather than traceable factory calibration. The expanded validation dataset — spanning two aircraft types, two flight phases, two microphones, and three controlled spectral references — confirms all comparisons fall within the ±2 dB IEC 61672 Class 2 unit-to-unit tolerance. A modest low-frequency sensitivity bias (~1.7 dB spread across spectral references) has been characterized and is consistent with observed aircraft measurement results. Windshield insertion loss has been confirmed within published industry specifications. The system provides a level of empirical confidence that exceeds what a manufacturer specification alone would offer, even without formal certification.
2
Sample size is small. Our validation dataset currently rests on a single matched A320 overflight. The methodology is sound regardless of sample size, but the empirical confidence interval narrows as we collect more comparisons. We will publish all future comparisons, including ones that disagree.
3
Measurements are displayed as 2-second peak-hold dBA. The reported value is the maximum A-weighted SPL observed during each 2-second window — closest in character to LAFmax (fast time-weighted maximum) in IEC 61672 terms. We do not currently report continuous LAeq integration over arbitrary periods.
4
We measure at the receptor, not at a standardized distance from the source. This is deliberate — receptor-based exposure is the variable health research uses — but it means our absolute SPL values cannot be directly compared to source-emission certifications like ICAO Annex 16 Chapter 14 noise levels, which are measured at fixed distances under controlled conditions.
5
Wind affects readings. Even with the fur windshield, sustained wind above ~5 m/s degrades accuracy. The app logs weather conditions alongside each measurement so wind-contaminated readings can be filtered in downstream analysis.
6
The calibration offset is empirically derived, not traceable to a national standards laboratory. A Class 1 reference (Brüel & Kjær 2250, NTi XL2) calibrated at a national metrology institute would be the next step up. Our TA657A reference is Class 2.
Section 9

Summary and data availability

All raw observation data collected by TrueNoise is freely available for download on the data page. This includes every column — including aircraft identifiers, operator names, and full psychoacoustic profiles — so that researchers and advocates can conduct their own independent analysis. We ask only for attribution.

As the measurement program expands to additional observers and locations, updated datasets will be published on a rolling basis. Methodology updates will be documented here with version notes.

We use the right algorithms (IEC 61672 A-weighting, ISO 532 loudness, DIN 45692 sharpness), running on hardware that is more than capable of executing them in real time, with a microphone whose response covers the relevant band, calibrated per-device against a Class 2 reference meter, with the calibration validated empirically on a real aircraft within Class 2 tolerance.

This is not a substitute for a Brüel & Kjær 2250. It is a measurement system whose accuracy is documented, whose methodology is transparent, and whose data is honest about its limitations. That is the standard we hold ourselves to. We invite anyone to hold us to it.
